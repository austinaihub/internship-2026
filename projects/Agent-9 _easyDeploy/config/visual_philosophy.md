# Visual Philosophy — Campaign Agent Imagery

This document defines the design principles for all generated campaign images.
It is referenced by the Audience Analyzer and Image Generator agents.

---

## Core Principle: Less Is More

A single powerful image with breathing room communicates more than a collage of symbols.
Every element in the frame must earn its place. When in doubt, remove.

---

## Print-Ready Quality Standard

Every image generated by this system is designed for **large-format poster printing**
(24×36 inches / 60×90 cm) and **gallery exhibition**. This means:

- **Tonal depth:** Images must have rich, continuous tonal gradations — no banding, no posterization.
  Think medium-format digital capture (Phase One, Hasselblad) or large-format film scan.
- **Micro-contrast:** Fine textures and material surfaces should be resolved with clarity.
  The viewer should feel they could touch the subject.
- **Color science:** Use cinematic color grading with intentional palette control.
  Reference: Kodak Portra 400, Fuji Pro 400H, or Kodachrome for warm subjects.
- **Print finish:** Images should look natural on museum-grade matte or baryta paper.
  Avoid digital artifacts, over-sharpening, or HDR glow.

---

## The Three Rules

### 1. One Subject, One Emotion
Each image has exactly **one** visual focal point and conveys **one** dominant emotion.
Do not layer multiple metaphors or scenes. A lone silhouette in a doorway is stronger
than a silhouette + scattered passports + barred windows + fog + neon signs.

### 2. Negative Space Is the Design
At least **50-60%** of the frame should be negative space — empty sky, blurred background,
solid shadow, soft gradient, or out-of-focus environment. This is not wasted space;
it is what gives the subject its weight and the composition its elegance.
Treat negative space as sacred geometry — every inch of emptiness is intentional.

### 3. Restrained Palette
Use **2-3 dominant tones** maximum. Let one color lead, one support, and use a subtle
accent sparingly. Monochromatic or near-monochromatic palettes often produce the
strongest results. Avoid saturated color variety within a single image.
Apply split-toning when appropriate: warm highlights paired with cool shadows for depth.

---

## Composition Guidelines

- **Simplicity over storytelling.** The image is not a narrative illustration — it is
  a single arresting moment frozen in time.
- **Shallow depth of field.** Keep the subject sharp and let the environment dissolve
  into soft bokeh. This naturally reduces visual clutter.
- **Geometric clarity.** Favor clean lines, symmetry, or deliberate rule-of-thirds
  placement. Avoid chaotic or busy compositions.
- **Frame-within-frame.** Use architectural or environmental elements to naturally
  frame the subject, adding depth and directing the eye.
- **Bottom 25% reserve.** Keep the lower quarter of the frame dark or uncluttered
  for text overlay — this doubles as a natural grounding element.

---

## What NOT To Do

- Do not include multiple symbolic objects (e.g., chains + passports + candles + broken locks).
  Pick the single strongest symbol.
- Do not crowd the frame with environmental detail. A hint of setting is enough.
- Do not use heavy-handed metaphors. Subtlety is more powerful.
- Do not generate images that look like stock photo collages or infographic illustrations.
- Do not describe more than one "scene" in a prompt.
- Do not produce images that look digitally generated — aim for photographic authenticity.

---

## Reference Aesthetic

Think: **Pentagram design studio** poster campaigns, **Saul Bass** title sequences,
**Amnesty International** award-winning awareness posters, **National Geographic**
photojournalism covers, **NYT Magazine** portrait features, **Apple** product photography,
**Magnum Photos** editorial work.

Clean, confident, with one clear point of focus and a sense of quiet gravity.
The kind of image that stops someone mid-scroll and demands a second look.
The kind of poster that belongs on a gallery wall.
